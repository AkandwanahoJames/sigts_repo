ABSTRACT: 
The vast advancements in telecommunications and converting medical information to a digital 
format have increased the number of applications within telemedicine. In Uganda, there has 
been increasing numbers of patients with skin diseases and yet there is a  limited number of 
dermatologists. One of the solutions for reaching an economically and socially viable solution 
to this challenge is exploiting the benefits of modern technologies.  
 
This report presents a solution to the challenge of limited dermatologi sts which is a mobile 
teledermatology application, the Snapskin Info-teledermatology Application that facilitates the 
consultation, the aftercare of patients and providing information about skin health to patients 
suffering from skin diseases. The application is based on store -and-forward teledermatology 
and consists of two main components: an application implemented on the mobile phone with 
two views: (1) For patients to transmit both skin image data and biofeedback information, (2) 
For dermatologists to work on the patients.   
 
A mobile application that allows sharing of photos showing different skin conditions by 
patients to dermatologists was developed. Through this the Dermatologist can give diagnosis 
and suggest the recommended prescriptions. This application was tested by Group 34 members 
to ensure that all features work efficiently. 
 
In conclusion therefore, the geographical and socioeconomic barriers preventing some patients 
from receiving dermatologic care and information have been minimized using teledermatology 
applications and systems and through addressing such obstacles improves the quality of 
healthcare services. 
 
Keywords: Application, Dermatology, Info -Teledermatology, Skin care, Skin diseases, 
Snapskin, Teledermatology, Telemedicine

the study, scope of the study and the significance of the study.  This study was aimed at

1.1 Background: 
Telemedicine is a service that is rapidly evolving to provide increased access to efficient, cost-
effective, and high-quality healthcare in Uganda and the world at large. It is defined as the use 
of devices along with technological resources to gain access to a patient and their health 
information to assess, evaluate, and diagnose them, as well as to decide if a visit to a healthcare 
center is required  (Giulio Nittari, 2022). It is also seen as a tool that can help manage the 
medical and financial risk of patient care in many parts of the world most e specially the 
underserved areas and as a result, providers with telemedicine capabilities are hoping to 
become more competitive in winning health care contracts which has led to many 
developments in the different sectors of telemedicine among which we have  teledermatology 
(Perednia, 1995). 
 
Teledermatology is a subspecialty of dermatology and probably among the most popular 
applications of e-health and telemedicine. The term “Teledermatology” was first coined by two 
dermatologists, Perednia and Brown in 1995 (Teledermatology: What Is It? Benefits of Virtual 
Dermatologist - eVisit, 2022). In this field, telecommunication technologies are being used to 
transfer medical information between the patients and the dermatologists over varying 
distances through audio , visual and data communication with an aim to provide skin care 
solutions to the patients with different skin issues. Teledermatology applications take on 
different areas of healthcare management such as consultation, diagnosis, treatment and even 
education about different skin diseases. 
 
Skin diseases (SD) are conditions that affect your skin, and these diseases may cause rashes, 
inflammation, itchiness, or other skin changes (Skin diseases: Types of, symptoms, treatment 
& prevention 2022). Some skin conditions may be genetic, while lifestyle factors may cause 
others, and these have proven to be a challenge in many communities. This has therefore called 
for intervention from different people and organizations. To mitigate this challenge, th e

1.2 Statement of the problem: 
In Uganda, there has been inaccessibility of dermatologists by patients and even more 
concerning, few people are aware that such services  exist. According to Dr. Leo Odongo, his 
previous training as a dermatologist enabled him to recognize the burden of skin conditions 
and infections within the local community ( Bringing Dermatology to the Underserved in 
Uganda, 2018). With few practicing dermatologists in Uganda, proper treatment has not been 
readily available and skin health care is limited due to lack of information about the skin 
diseases amongst primary healthcare workers, limited access to dermatologists, lack of proper 
training of healthcare workers and shortage of appropriate treatments. 
 
The demand for dermatologic care is increasing, and this apparent undersupply of 
dermatologists may be contributing to disparities in access to skin health care. One of the 
largest barriers to accessing dermatologic care is inaccessibility of Dermatologists and 
difficulty obtaining appointments due to long waiting time (Maddukuri et al., 2021). Therefore, 
the Snapskin Info -Teledermatology Application serves  a purpose in bridging this gap, 
increasing access to dermatology care and information to meet these unmet needs of the people 
of Uganda.

2.0 Introduction:

2.1 Telemedicine 
Telemedicine can be defined as a new technology which provides medical information and 
remote health care support or a modern technology supporting health care at a distance (Pala 
et al., 2020)  According to the World Health Organization, Telemedicine is de fined as the 
delivery of healthcare services, with distance as a critical factor, whereby all health care 
professionals use information and communication technologies for the exchange of valid 
information for diagnosis, treatment and prevention of diseases  and injuries, research and 
evaluation, and for the continuing education of health care providers, all in the interests of 
advancing the health of individuals and their communities (ISSA, 2021). 
 
2.1.1 Forms of Telemedicine: 
There are various forms of Telemedicine and these include: 
Telepharmacy is a form of pharmaceutical care in which pharmacists and patients are not in 
the same place and can interact using information and communication technology (ICT) 
facilities (Baldoni, 2019).  
Telepathology is the practice of remote pathology using telecommunication links to enable the 
electronic transmission of digital pathology images (Farahani & Pantanowitz). 
Telepsychiatry is a form of telemedicine that uses telephone or video conferencing tools to 
provide psychiatric services (Leonard, 2020). 
Telecardiology refers to the telemonitoring of patients with an IMD as part of the management 
of a cardiac pathology (treatment or diagnosis): pacemaker, implantable cardiac defibrillator 
or long-term rhythmic holter (Science-Direct, 2022). 
Telesurgery, also called remote surgery, is performed by a surgeon at a site remote from the 
patient and the s urgical tasks are directly performed by a robotic system controlled by the 
surgeon at the remote site (Encyclopedia, 2018).

2.2 Teledermatology 
Teledermatology (commonly referred to as Telederm by practitioners) is one of the most 
common telemedicine applications (Friis, 2022) and is defined as a subspecialty of 
dermatology which is the branch of medicine  concerned with the diagnosis, treatment, and 
prevention of diseases of the skin, hair, nails, oral cavity, and genitals (Davis, 2021).   
 
The increase in use of teledermatology  technologies has been due to the many patients not 
having access to appropriate dermatological care and the upward trend in the skin cancers 
morbidity. Nowadays smartphones, computers and other Internet connected devices are 
commonly used in teledermatolo gy since they are cheaper and more portable and since skin 
disorders in majority of cases are easily seen by the human eye therefore can be accurately 
captured by imaging technologies (Pala et al., 2020). 
 
Applications of teledermatology span different mod es of healthcare management such as 
consultation where a patient with a certain skin condition contacts a dermatologist through 
telecommunication to request proper diagnosis and treatment (evisit, 2022), triage  which is a 
process of prioritizing patients and determining the need for in-person visits, direct-care where 
patients are able to take photos of their skin lesions and send them directly to their physicians 
and Follow -up which involves remote medical supervision of chronic skin conditions and 
optimize therapy (Pala et al., 2020). 
 
These modes can be achieved by using the different areas of teledermatology that include; -
telediagnosis which serves as a triage tool, guiding individuals to the appropriate health care 
provider for his or her disease, speci alist referral  where medical professional or general 
practitioner who sees the patient can consult a specialist through telecommunication in order 
to receive a second opinion and home telehealth, or telehomecare which involves the treatment 
of a patient wi th a chronic condition remotely at home. A critical aspect of interest of 
telehomecare when it comes to dermatology deals with patients with skin conditions that 
require follow-up appointments (evisit, 2022).

2.4 Modalities of Teledermatology. 
Teledermatology has two established modalities: store -and-forward (asynchronous) and live 
interaction (synchronous).  
 
Store and Forward Modality. 
Store-and-forward teledermatology  consists of an electronic platform where referring 
healthcare providers upload digital pictures including dermatoscopic images. These images, 
combined with pertinent information on the patient’s medical history, are transmitted in 
encrypted format to dermatologists who provide a diagnosis and treatment plan. On the other 
hand, the live interaction modality involves real -time video interaction between the primary 
care provider and the tele -dermatologist. Both the store -and-forward and live interaction 
modalities can be applied between referring physicians and consulting dermatologists or 
directly between patients and dermatologists (Campagna, et al., 2017).

2.7 Factors Affecting Teledermatology  
Authors from the Departure of Dermatology in New York distinguished five different 
categories of factors: clinical, economic, technological, legal, and ethical (Pala et al., 2020).  
 
Clinical 
Clinical considerations are specifically important and account for proper diagnosis and the 
following treatment process.  The accuracy of teledermatology might be disputed when 
diagnosing dermatoses such as psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, and actinic keratosis. 
Teledermatology does not allow palpation while it ha s been proven that the physical touch is 
an important asset when examining a patient, equally as crucial as visual diagnosing. (Cox, 
2007). 
 
Some conditions are not perfectly suited for the store and forward model. For instance, cases 
when full body examin ation is needed, patients with naevus are in the high -risk group of 
developing melanoma or patients require in -person counseling due to extensive medical 
records.  
Patients seeking clarification, in case they have questions regarding recommendations, might  
encounter difficulties with getting timely feedback. The lack of information exchange can

inhibit development and improvement of teledermatology programs.  When it comes to 
assessing pigmented lesions, general practitioners relying only on teledermatology  may risk 
under detection of melanomas. In order to take the necessary precautions in this health care 
model, both dermatologists and non-dermatologists must be willing to conduct full -body skin 
examinations. 
 
Economic 
The most frequently named economic ch allenge is obtaining reimbursements. There is a 
widespread concern that consultations delivered through teledermatology are reimbursed at a 
lower rate compared to in -person visits. Most mentioned concerns are delays or even lack of 
any refunds in cases when the patient has failed to obtain the services. While many insurance 
policies, including Medicare, often offer coverage for teledermatology services, there are still 
several insurances that do not  reimburse for this type of care. This can be a major barri er for 
teledermatology (Pala et al., 2020). 
 
Technological 
Most frequently mentioned challenges in teledermatology are technological barriers. Evidence 
suggests that poor image quality, billing systems integration and inefficient, expensive 
software are ar eas which require a lot of improvement.  Smartphones and commercially 
available telemedicine platforms remain expensive, thus there is a need to make them 
affordable and compatible with the existing medical record systems (Pala et al., 2020). Reliance 
on sm artphone-captured images may be misleading because  their cameras are of inferior 
quality and the health care workforce lack formal photography training. All these issues impair 
the ability to provide high-quality and timely care. 
 
Legal 
Main considerations relate to security of patients’ information and medical data transmitted 
electronically. In the Position Statement on Telemedicine made by the American Aca demy of 
Dermatology Association (Pala et al., 2020), there is a record that providers should encrypt 
transmissions to secure health information, and they should include safety understanding in a 
consent form. Information should be stored on firewall -protected servers. Patients should be 
properly informed of who has access, how information is stored along with all its benefits and 
risks.

Support for Teledermatology is especially applicable in underserved areas. Providers and

3.1 Research design 
The research design refers to the overall strategy you choose to integrate the different 
components of the study in a coherent and logical way, thereby  ensuring you will effectively 
address the research problem ( Ridzuan et al,2021 ). This study adopted a descriptive cross -
sectional design which involved looking at data at one specific point in time (Kesmodel, 2018). 
This is because the study involved looking at different characteristics for example age, income, 
gender among others of the participants and also enabled us to obtain information about the 
ease of access of dermatological information and services. 
 
This study used data gathering approaches such a s questionnaires, interviews, existing 
documents, and surveys to collect both qualitative and quantitative data.  The use of the 
qualitative data informed the researcher about people’s feelings, thoughts, and beliefs (Sutton 
& Austin, 2015) while the quantitative approach helped the researcher verify the hypothesis 
meaning for purposes of better understanding of the study variables.

4.1.1 Current Study 
The current system of accessing Dermatology services in Uganda is both physical and contact 
based. Currently, hospitals in Uganda allow patients to book appointments either physically at 
the hospital premises or via a call to the hospital help desk. 
Some hospitals have websites  that are accessed by a number of people, and these provide 
general information about general doctors, how to access ambulances, and other hospital 
services. However, due to lack of dermatology services on some of these websites, patients are 
compelled to search for dermatologists’ contacts on th e internet, or from other sources, and 
make calls that are sometimes received by receptionists who help to make the appointments 
depending on the dermatologists’ working schedule. 
 
4.1.2 Strength of the Current System 
The current system has various strengths as explained below. 
The system allows the patients to physically access the hospital premises where the 
dermatologist diagnoses and prescribes the required medication according to the skin disease. 
This mode provides accurate diagnosis to the patient.

accessible at any time. 
 
4.1.3 Weaknesses of the Current system 
The system is time-consuming, and patients travel to meet dermatologists even those with 
minor skin diseases that can be prescribed without physically seeing the dermatologist. 
The system provides limited information about dermatology services and skincare. It does not 
provide enough information about the various skin diseases, most common and deadly skin 
diseases, how to prevent them and possible ways for one to take care of their skin from such

CHAPTER FIVE: SYSTEM IMPLEMENTATION, TESTING AND VALIDATION. 
5.0 Introduction 
The chapter defines the implementation of the design to meet the requirements of the system 
as well as testing and validation of the system. 
 
5.1 System Implementation 
Different technologies were used during the implementation of this application as they are 
further explained below. 
 
5.1.1  System technologies 
The Snapskin Info-Teledermatology Application was developed with the aid of React Native 
which is an open-source user-interface software framework created by Meta Platforms, Inc. It 
was used to develop applications for Android by enabling developers to use the React 
framework along with native platform capabilities. 
 
HyperText Markup Language (HTML) was used as a language that specified the structure of 
the document retrieval across the internet using browser programs on the World Wide Web. 
JavaScript (JS), which is also an easy -to-use open-source client-side scripting language, was 
used to develop dynamic functionalities of the system. Its ability to display information in the 
browsers enables users to use the system. 
 
Firebase is a platform developed by Google for creating mobile and web applications. It  is a 
Backend-as-a-Service (BaaS) app development platform that provides hosted backend services 
such as a real-time database, cloud storage, authentication, crash reporting, machine learning, 
remote configuration, and hosting for your static files.  Most of the software used in the 
application implementation were chosen because it was cheap, readily available, and supported 
in rapid development. 
 
5.2 System Functionality

The system allows administrators to log in before accessing the Dashboard page to carry out 
the administrative roles. On the other hand, the system requires the users, that is the patients 
and the dermatologists, to login before accessing the system but requires verification of the

CHAPTER SIX: SUMMARY, LIMITATION, CONCLUSION AND 
RECOMMENDATIONS 
6.0 Introduction: 
This chapter provides a summary, limitation, conclusion, and recommendations of the 
Snapskin Info-teledermatology application. 
 
6.1 Achievements: 
The Snapskin Info -teledermatology application has ensured easy access to dermatologists, 
Skincare services, Information about skin diseases and healthy skin follow up by patients. 
 
6.2 Challenges: 
 The Snapskin Info-teledermatology application is intended to be installed on smartphones and 
used when there is internet access. As such, it requires an internet connection. The system shall 
not be able to function if the server stops running. The server should always run when the 
system is loading. 
 
6.3 Limitation of the study: 
The project needed a variety of resources that is to say funds to facilitate the data collection 
and the computer system for developing the application which were not easy to get, and it was 
difficult for us to get dermatologists to interview due to the limited number of dermatologists 
and they are always occupied since they have to meet very many patients. 
 
6.4 Recommendations: 
Snapskin Info-teledermatology application is highly recommended for use by Skin patients and 
dermatologists because its aim is to reduce the gap between dermatologists and the skin 
patients. It is also important to train and encourage users to get accustomed to the Snapskin 
Info-teledermatology application. This being a new application, some of the users (patients) 
may need to fully understand how the application functions. 
 
6.5 Conclusions:  
The core reason for the establishment of The Snapskin Info -teledermatology application is to 
reduce the gap between skin patients and dermatologists within Kampala. Therefore, the 
technologies used should   support the core objective of the system if it is to remain relevant to 
the users. A lot still needs to be done in the IT d epartment to make available technology
