ABSTRACT:
Visitors to Bwindi Impenetrable National Park often rely on tour guides and lodge staff for facts about trails, fees, wildlife, and local culture. When those sources disagree or age without update, tourists leave uncertain about what is accurate. This project built the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) to host Uganda Wildlife Authority–reviewed content on a park intranet that smartphones can reach before and during a visit, including short periods without mobile data.
The work pursued one general objective: to deliver a tested prototype that stores organised, verifiable information for tourists and guides. Tasks covered requirements from interviews and questionnaires, database and screen design, coding with Next.js, Express.js, PostgreSQL/PostGIS, and Redis, and validation with park stakeholders.
The team followed Agile sprints from January to June 2026. Field data showed that half of surveyed visitors ranked information accuracy as their main difficulty, with smaller groups citing weak online material, pricing confusion, and language limits. Acceptance testing produced a mean usability rating of 4.2 on a five-point scale, and every case in Table 5.2 passed on the release candidate.
SIGTS can support Bwindi operations if content editors refresh facts each season and guides treat the tool as a backup to spoken explanation, not a replacement. The authors advise a longer pilot with Uganda Safari Guides Association members, fuller Luganda material, and better signal at park gates.

1.2 STATEMENT OF THE PROBLEM
Tourists in Uganda reported receiving inconsistent, shallow, or contradictory information from their guides during their visits (Mbago, 2024). This has led to widespread tourist confusion and dissatisfaction, which diminishes the overall visitor experience and appreciation of the country's tourism products (Mbago, 2024). The root cause of this problem is the lack of proper training and professional development among tour guides, many of whom lack the knowledge and ethical principles required for their role (Mbago, 2024). Other contributing causes include the absence of a standardized national certification system for guides, limited access to up-to-date informational resources, and insufficient continuous learning opportunities. 
While sector associations like the Uganda Safari Guides Association (USAGA) have emphasized this issue, their efforts have been insufficient to create sector-wide professionalism (Mbago, 2024). The lack of reliable information has also resulted in a decline in Uganda's international tourism image, a reduction in repeat visits, and ultimately, lower revenue generation for the sector (Mbago, 2024). 
However, even if a tour guide possesses certified training and authentic information resources, the integrity of the information delivered to the tourist was no longer guaranteed at the point of interaction. This was because guides often relied on personal, outdated information where information could be easily misinterpreted or sourced from unverified platforms.  Existing systems such as SmartGuide, TripAdvisor and MakeMyTrip provide digital tourism information, they largely rely on generalized content and lack site-specific validation and offline support. Therefore, it was critical to develop an integrated smart information guide tour system that delivers verified, standardized and context-aware information ensuring information accuracy and consistency when accessed through common consumer devices in the field.

1.5 RESEARCH SCOPE
1.5.1 Geographical Scope
This study primarily focused on Bwindi Impenetrable National Park since “there have been efforts to extend Information and Communication Technology (ICT) connectivity to tourist sites such as Bwindi and Murchison Falls” (NDPIV, 2025), located in southwestern Uganda, on the edge of the Rift Valley. Bwindi is a United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) World Heritage Site and one of the country’s most iconic tourist destinations, renowned globally for its rich biodiversity and as a sanctuary for nearly half of the world’s remaining mountain gorillas (MoTWA, 2023). The Park covers approximately 321 square kilometres of dense montane and lowland forest, providing a unique ecological setting that attracts thousands of local and international tourists each year (MoTWA, 2023).
1.5.2 Content Scope
This study systematically developed and validated a Smart Information Guide Tour System prototype. First, requirements were gathered through stakeholder surveys and interviews. These requirements informed us of the design of a client-server architecture optimized for intranet use. The implementation focused on core modules: a content management system for verified information and a tourist interface with intelligent offline search. The prototype was rigorously tested and validated for functionality and usability to demonstrate improved information access.
1.5.3 Time Scope
This study was executed within a seven-month timeframe (January–June 2026) using an Agile software development methodology. Work was organized into iterative sprints with continuous stakeholder feedback, not a rigid sequential (Waterfall) lifecycle.
Sprint 0 (Month 1 — Discovery and Requirements): Structured interviews, tourist questionnaires, and document review were conducted to gather and document system specifications for the product backlog.
Sprints 1–2 (Months 2–3 — Design): The client-server architecture, PostgreSQL database schema, UML diagrams (DFDs, EERD, flowcharts), and Figma prototypes were developed and refined through sprint reviews.
Sprints 3–5 (Months 3–5 — Development): Core modules were built iteratively, including the content management system, tourist interface, offline search, authentication, and AI-assisted query features.
Sprint 6 (Month 6 — Testing, Validation, and Release): Unit, integration, usability, and user acceptance testing were conducted. The integrated prototype was validated for functionality, usability, and improved information access before documentation and deployment.

2.2 TYPES AND CLASSIFICATION OF THE PROPOSED SYSTEM
2.2.1 Types of Information Systems
Information systems are classified based on the level of management they support, the nature of tasks they perform, and the kind of information they process. The major types include Transaction Processing Systems (TPS), Decision Support Systems (DSS), Executive Information Systems (EIS), Office Automation Systems (OAS), Expert Systems (ES), and Management Information Systems (MIS) (Laudon & Laudon, 2023).
Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)
These systems handle and record the day-to-day business transactions of an organization such as sales, bookings, payments, or registrations. They provide accurate, timely data that serves as input for higher-level systems (O’Brien and Marakas, 2021).Example: Hotel booking systems and point-of-sale systems.
Decision Support Systems (DSS)
DSS assist managers in making complex, semi-structured decisions by analyzing large data sets and providing simulations or projections (Power, 2021).Example: A system that helps tourism managers forecast visitor demand based on past data and environmental conditions.
Executive Information Systems (EIS)
These systems are designed for senior executives to monitor organizational performance and strategic goals. They offer visual dashboards and summary reports for quick decision-making (Laudon and Laudon, 2023).Example: A tourism authority dashboard showing national park performance indicators.
Office Automation Systems (OAS)
OAS support routine office functions such as document management, scheduling, communication, and coordination among staff members (O’Brien and Marakas, 2021).Example: An email and document-sharing platform for a tourism department.
Expert Systems (ES)
Expert Systems use artificial intelligence to mimic human expertise in specialized domains, helping users make informed decisions (Turban et al., 2022).Example: An Artificial Intelligent system recommending tourism packages based on traveller preferences.
Management Information Systems (MIS)
Management Information Systems (MIS) are designed to collect, process, and present information that supports middle-level management in planning, monitoring, and controlling operations. MIS provide summarized and structured reports derived from raw data generated by Transaction Processing Systems (Laudon and Laudon, 2023).They improve managerial efficiency by supplying accurate, timely, and relevant information for decision-making, performance monitoring, and strategic adjustments.
2.2.1.1 Classification of the Proposed System
The Smart Information Guide Tour System falls under the Management Information System (MIS) category. It gathers and manages data related to tourist activities, destinations, and feedback, and then processes it to generate useful insights for park managers and tour administrators. The system’s predictive analytics and reporting features align with Management Information System (MIS) functions transforming operational data into meaningful reports that guide decisions on visitor management, tour planning, and resource allocation.
Moreover, the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) enhances the traditional Management Information System (MIS) capabilities by automating data analysis, generating reports, and supporting adaptive decision-making within the park’s management framework. Thus, Smart Information Guide Tour System operates as a Tourism-Oriented Management Information System, designed to enhance both managerial oversight and visitor experience.

2.2.3 The Proposed System
The Smart Information Guide Tour System is an intelligent, intranet-based tourism information platform designed to revolutionize how tourists access and experience information within Bwindi Impenetrable National Park. Built to bridge the persistent information gap between tour guides and tourists the system ensures that tourists receive accurate, up-to-date, and context-rich knowledge about attractions, wildlife, cultural heritage, and park activities in real-time.
Unlike traditional guiding methods that depend heavily on human narration and printed brochures, Smart Information Guide Tour System harnesses the power of artificial intelligence, predictive analytics and smart navigation to deliver a personalized, data-driven experience even in remote areas with limited internet connectivity. Operating on a secure intranet environment, it guarantees continuous functionality, offline data access, and local content verification, making it a dependable digital companion for both tourists and guides.
By seamlessly integrating modules for artificial intelligence content generation, predictive visitor insights, cultural storytelling, and customized feedback loops, the system transforms Bwindi into a smart destination hub a model of how Information Systems and Technology can sustainably enhance tourism, education, and conservation in Uganda and beyond (UNWTO, 2024 and Jamshidi et al., 2023).
2.2.3.1 Detailed Description of the proposed system features 
1.  Intranet-Based Infrastructure
The system operates on a secure intranet, ensuring uninterrupted access even in areas with limited or no internet connectivity. This local network architecture allows all guides, kiosks, and tourist devices within the park to connect and share data seamlessly.According to Al-Okaily (2021), intranet systems enhance information reliability and security by restricting access to authorized users while maintaining high data transfer speeds. This ensures that tourists can still browse maps, cultural data, and narratives without relying on external internet providers, a crucial feature for remote destinations like Bwindi Impenetrable.Key benefit: Real-time local connectivity, faster data retrieval, and guaranteed availability of verified park content.
2.   Artificial Intelligence (AI) Integration
The system integrates AI capabilities through a chatbot to enhance information processing, user engagement, and system adaptability. This artificial intelligence component was implemented as follows:
Natural language queries, allowing tourists to ask questions and receive instant responses.
The tour-help feature is implemented as a Next.js page (ask the AI chatbot) that is designed to call POST /api/tourist/ai-query once the backend route is completed. In the pilot, tourists receive answers from curated FAQs and animals/cultural_narratives content through existing GET routes; the AI assistant screen demonstrates the intended chat workflow. Park-approved rows in PostgreSQL remain the authoritative knowledge base; a future release will add retrieval-augmented prompting once the Express handler is wired.
Studies show that Artificial Intelligence (AI)-driven systems increase personalization, reduce decision fatigue, and improve satisfaction (Chakraborty et al., 2022). In Smart Information Guide Tour System, AI ensures that information is not only available but also context-aware and adaptive to tourist needs.
3.  Predictive Analysis and Reporting
Predictive analytics in the pilot build did not use an opaque black-box model. Inputs are anonymized rows from tour_sessions, sightings, and feedback tables. Peak-hour congestion is forecast with a seven-day moving average of hourly gate check-ins per sector, computed nightly in PostgreSQL using window functions (AVG() OVER (PARTITION BY sector_id ORDER BY hour_bucket)). An alert is raised when the forecast exceeds 85% of the capacity value stored in park_sectors. Route popularity ranks use COUNT(*) on sightings grouped by location_id for the IT dashboard. Satisfaction trends aggregate Likert scores from the tourist questionnaire linked in Chapter 4. These SQL-based metrics feed the analytics API (GET /api/admin/analytics) and exportable CSV reports for park managers.
This feature assists park management in:
Predicting peak visiting hours.
Identifying areas of tourist congestion.
Generating dynamic reports on visitor satisfaction and site performance.
According to Seraj et al. (2025), predictive tourism analytics improve decision-making by transforming raw visitor data into actionable insights, promoting better planning and resource allocation.Key benefit: Enhances operational efficiency, supports conservation management, and enables data-driven decision-making.
4.  Smart Navigation
The system integrates an intelligent navigation module that provides both map-based route assistance within the park. Using geolocation technologies (operating offline through the intranet), tourists can view routes and receive directional guidance.This feature improves visitor safety and orientation while reducing dependence on physical tour maps or constant guide supervision. Kang et al. (2020) emphasize that smart navigation systems significantly enhance user autonomy and confidence in unfamiliar destinations.Key benefit: Improves accessibility, safety, and independence in tourist movement within the park.
5.  In-Tour Information
At the core of the Smart Information Guide Tour System is in-tour information delivery providing tourists with accurate, site-specific data as they explore various park zones.This includes dynamic facts about flora, fauna, conservation history, and ongoing projects displayed on devices or interactive kiosks. According to Mbago (2024), inadequate in-tour information remains a major source of tourist dissatisfaction in Uganda’s parks. By delivering structured and multimedia-supported content (text, images, and voice notes), the system ensures tourists remain informed, engaged, and educated throughout the journey.Key benefit: Enhances learning, engagement, and satisfaction during the actual tour experience.
6.  Cultural Narratives
Recognizing Bwindi’s deep cultural details, the system includes a cultural narratives feature. This is a digital storytelling module that shares indigenous traditions, myths, and heritage linked to the local communities surrounding the park for example the Batwa. This feature supports cultural preservation and tourism education, aligning with the UNWTO (2024) call for technology-driven cultural inclusion in tourism. 
Key benefit: Promotes cultural appreciation, supports local identity, and enriches the visitor experience through immersive storytelling.
7.  General Destination Information
The General Destination Information section serves as a digital encyclopedia for Bwindi and its surroundings, covering topics such as park entry fees, accommodation, weather updates, conservation rules, and emergency contacts. According to Sun et al. (2022), tourists often prioritize access to accurate logistical data when planning and navigating destinations. This module ensures that visitors have all essential pre-tour and on-site information, minimizing confusion and improving preparedness. 
Key benefit: Centralized access to all vital park and travel data in one interface.
8.  Customization and Feedback
The Customization and Feedback module allows users to personalize their experiences and provide direct input to park authorities. Tourists can rate tours, suggest improvements, and customize the system’s interface to match their preferences (language, theme, content depth).Feedback data was designed to feed into AI algorithms for continuous system learning and improvement. Goo et al. (2022) note that such participatory feedback mechanisms promote tourist trust and long-term loyalty. 
Key benefit: Promotes user engagement, continuous improvement, and responsive service delivery.

Policy direction from NDPIV and NITA-U supports digitisation, while field evidence points to inconsistent guide narratives as the practical problem. SIGTS responds by tying content approval to park management, storing data for offline use, and recording sightings and feedback that administrators can audit later.
CHAPTER THREE: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY
3.0 Introduction
This chapter presents the methods and procedures for designing, developing, testing, and validating the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS). It outlines the research design, data collection methods, data analysis procedures, and the Agile system development process adopted for this project. The methodology ensures systematic development, validation by real users, and alignment with the project objectives.
3.1 Research Design
The Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) was developed using an Agile software development methodology. Agile supports iterative delivery, continuous stakeholder feedback, and adaptation to changing requirements which is essential for tourism platforms where visitor needs and destination information evolve frequently. The project used time-boxed sprints, a prioritized product backlog, sprint planning, stakeholder reviews, and retrospectives to improve each cycle. A pure Waterfall lifecycle was not used because tourism requirements evolved with stakeholder feedback; Agile sprints allowed iterative design, implementation, and testing.
3.1.1 Agile Development Phases and Application to the Project
Requirements Gathering and Analysis: Structured interviews, online tourist questionnaires, and document review were conducted to identify functional and non-functional requirements. Stakeholders included tourists, tour guides, park managers, and developers. Google Forms and interview guides were used. The output was a prioritized product backlog and requirements specification.
Planning: The team defined system modules, the content management module, user interaction module, reporting dashboards, and AI module and broke work into sprint backlogs. Sprint durations were set, tasks assigned, and deliverables scheduled. Trello and Gantt charts supported planning, producing an approved sprint plan aligned with Agile milestones.
System Design: SIGTS architecture was modelled using a structured, process-centred approach. Data flow diagrams, entity-relationship diagrams, context diagrams, and flowcharts visualized processes, data stores, and external entities. Draw.io, Figma, and PostgreSQL schema scripts in backend/sql/init were used. Outputs were reviewed in sprint reviews and refined before implementation.
Software tools such as Draw.io, Figma, and PostgreSQL migration scripts were used. The expected output was a complete system design specification document.
Development (Implementation): Developers built the system iteratively with front-end interfaces, back-end APIs, database integration, authentication, and core SIGTS workflows. Technologies included Next.js 14, React 18, Tailwind CSS, Node.js, Express.js, PostgreSQL with PostGIS, and Redis. Each sprint delivered working increments toward a functional prototype.
Testing and Validation: Unit, integration, usability, and user acceptance testing ran within and after development sprints. Tourists and guides tested features in realistic scenarios. Postman, test scripts, and browser developer tools supported verification. The output was a tested, validated system meeting acceptance criteria.
Release and Documentation: The team deployed the system, finalized documentation, and collected user feedback. Web hosting, GitHub, and Render supported deployment. This phase delivered the working SIGTS prototype and complete project documentation.
3.2 Data Collection Methods and Procedures
To gather system requirements and understand user needs, qualitative and quantitative data collection methods were used. Methods, instruments, sample sizes, and procedures are outlined below.
3.2.1 Interviews
Procedure
Structured interviews were conducted with key stakeholders from relevant user groups including park managers, tour guides and park staff. Participants were contacted beforehand, appointments scheduled, and each session lasted approximately 20–30 minutes. Interviews were conducted face-to-face.
Purpose
To understand current nature-based information-sharing processes between guides, tourists, and park management, identify key operational challenges in delivering accurate and engaging content, assess the security and data privacy needs of a localized system, and determine the preferred user interaction channels for example kiosk and mobile web
Instrument: Interview Guide (attached in the appendices)
3.2.2 Questionnaires (Online Surveys)
Procedure
Google Forms questionnaires were distributed to tourists (local and international) visiting or planning to visit Bwindi Impenetrable National Park. Links were shared through tour operators, park visitor centres, lodges, and relevant social media or travel forums. Responses were collected automatically in Google Sheets for descriptive and inferential analysis.
Purpose
To gather large-scale quantitative data on user preferences, including preferred information access method (kiosk and personal mobile device), desired types of content (wildlife facts, trail maps, safety and privacy of information), frequency of seeking information during visits, and overall interest in and acceptance of an AI-assisted information system.
Instrument: Questionnaire Form (attached in the appendices)
3.2.3 Document Review
Procedure
A systematic review of existing Artificial Intelligence-driven information systems, tour guide platforms, and nature interpretation technologies was conducted. Examples include systems like SmartGuide, TripAdvisor, MakeMyTrip, Explore Uganda and various museum/kiosk-based interactive guides. Their system architectures, user interaction models, content delivery methods, and approaches to offline functionality and data privacy was designed to be examined to benchmark relevant best practices and technological approaches.
Purpose
To understand the capabilities and limitations of existing solutions, identify design and functionality gaps in the current ecosystem, and inform the technical and experiential design of the proposed Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) to ensure it offers distinctive value.
Instrument: Document Review Checklist

A purposive sample was used: 15–20 professionals for in-depth requirements (qualitative depth) and a target of 150+ tourists for questionnaire data (quantitative breadth). 3.2.5 Ethical Considerations and Data Privacy
Ethical approval for this study was obtained in accordance with university research guidelines and standards governing academic research involving human participants. Participation in the study was entirely voluntary, and all respondents provided informed consent before taking part in interviews or completing questionnaires.
Data collection involved both physical interviews and online questionnaires administered through Google Forms. The online survey targeted tourists and individuals who had previously visited any tourist site, including secondary school students, university students, and members of the general public. The questionnaire link was voluntarily shared with participants through digital communication platforms like WhatsApp and respondents independently chose whether to participate in the study. No participant was pressured or financially induced to provide responses.
To ensure confidentiality and privacy, no personally identifiable information was collected or disclosed in the research findings. All responses were anonymized and used strictly for academic and system development purposes. Furthermore, the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) was designed with strong data protection measures, including role-based access control, encrypted authentication, secure intranet communication, and data minimization principles. These measures were implemented in compliance with Uganda’s Data Protection and Privacy Act and relevant park operational policies to safeguard tourist and operational information from unauthorized access or misuse.
3.2.6 Risk Analysis
The key risks identified included unreliable internet connectivity within remote forest areas, which will be mitigated through offline caching and intranet-based service delivery. The risk of inaccurate or misleading AI-generated responses will be reduced through Uganda Wildlife Authority-approved information sources. Potential low user adoption among tourists and tour guides will be addressed through stakeholder sensitization, user training and intuitive system design. Risks associated with unauthorized access and data breaches will be mitigated using intranet isolation, role-based access control (RBAC), encrypted authentication mechanisms and continuous system monitoring to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of tourism and operational data.

4.2 System Study
This phase of the project involved studying the operations and procedures of the current tourism information and tour management systems to identify their strengths and weaknesses. During this study, data collection methods such as interviews and questionnaires, both online and physical, were used to gather information from tourists, tour guides, park managers, and IT Manager.
The study was conducted to identify the challenges faced in accessing tourism information, navigation, wildlife tracking, and tour management services within the park environment, and to propose the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) as a solution. The information collected from potential system users was critically analyzed and evaluated to establish the system objectives and user requirements. 
4.2.1 Current Study
The current system of accessing tourism information at most tourist sites in Uganda is largely manual and guide-based. Tourists mainly rely on physical tour guides and onsite information centers to obtain details about attractions, wildlife, cultural heritage, and park activities.
Although some tourist sites and national parks provide basic information through websites or brochures, the information is often limited, outdated, or not detailed enough to support real-time visitor needs. As a result, tourists frequently depend on verbal explanations from tour guides, which may vary in consistency and accuracy.
4.2.2 Strength of the current system
The current tourism information system has several strengths as outlined below.
The system allows tourists to physically interact with tour guides at the site, enabling them to receive direct explanations and detailed interpretations of attractions, wildlife, and cultural heritage. This face-to-face interaction improves understanding and provides a more personalized experience for visitors.
The presence of onsite information centers and printed materials such as brochures provides tourists with basic guidance about the site, expected activities, and available services. These resources can be accessed without requiring internet connectivity, making them useful in remote areas.
The current system supports experiential learning, as tourists gain firsthand knowledge through guided tours and real-time demonstrations within the park environment.
4.2.3 Weakness of the current system
The current tourism information system has several limitations that affect the quality and efficiency of information delivery to visitors.
The system is time consuming, as tourists are required to physically engage tour guides at every point of interest, even for minor or general information that could be accessed digitally. This often leads to delays and reduces overall visitor convenience.
 The system provides limited and sometimes inconsistent information about attractions, wildlife, cultural heritage, and park services. Tourists may not receive sufficient details about sites, conservation practices, or historical background, which affects their overall experience and learning.
It is difficult to ensure continuous and standardized information delivery, as tour guides may differ in interpretation and presentation. This can result in variation in the quality and accuracy of information provided to different visitors.

4.5 SYSTEM DESIGN
4.5.1 System Architecture for the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS)
4.5.1.1 Introduction to the Tiered Architecture
Following Singh (2024), who define “tiered architecture as the physical separation of presentation, application, and data management functions into distinct layers that communicate through well-defined interfaces,” the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) implements a three-tier architecture with offline-first capabilities. This architectural pattern is specifically designed to address the unique challenges of Bwindi Impenetrable National Park, where cellular coverage is limited to less than 30% of the park’s 331 square kilometres.
According to Silberschatz et al. (2020), “the three-tier architecture provides scalability, maintainability, and security by separating user interface logic from business rules and data storage.” For SIGTS, this separation enables:
Independent scaling of each tier based on demand (500+ concurrent users during peak season)
Enhanced security through tiered access controls
Offline-first operation through client-side data caching
Geospatial optimization using PostGIS at the data tier
4.5.1.2 Design for Smart Information Guide Tour System
The design of the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS). Followed the three-level database architecture framework established by Singh (2024), who conducted a systematic literature review concluding that “three levels of representation are distinguished in databases which are, conceptual, logical, and physical data models.” The Bwindi Smart Information Guide Tour System context, the conceptual model serves as the blueprint that captures the park’s operational reality, tourists accessing information, guides leading tours, and administrators managing content without concern for implementation details.
The Enhanced Entity-Relationship (EER) methodology applied to SIGTS follows Al-Fedaghi (2021), who demonstrated that “Entity-Relationship models can be extended as ‘Thinging Machines’ to capture complex business domains with specialization, generalization, and categorization hierarchies.” This is particularly relevant for Bwindi, where the system must distinguish between three distinct user types, TOURIST (visitors accessing information), TOUR GUIDE (professional guides managing tours), and IT MANAGER (park administrators) all sharing common attributes (username, password, contanct information) through inheritance from a user superclass. This specialization hierarchy, represented in the EEERD ensures that each user count belongs to exactly one role while inheriting base attributes, reflecting the real-world organizational structure of Bwindi Impenetrable National Park.
The identification of 35 entities in the SIGTS conceptual model aligns with the systematic mapping review of Vera-Olivera et al. (2021), which found that “conceptual and logical levels received more attention than the physical level of representation, with UML, ER, and new notations based on ER being adapted to model complex databases.” For Bwindi Impenetrable National Park, these entities span seven functional categories: core user management (USER, TOURIST, TOUR GUIDE, IT MANAGER), geographic structure (PARK, LOCATION), wildlife (ANIMAL, SIGHTING for tracking the mountain gorillas and bird species), cultural narratives (CULTURAL NARRATIVE), tour management (TOUR ROUTE, TOUR SESSION, TOUR PARTICIPANT), navigation (NAVIGATION PATH for offline routing), and system support (SYNC QUEUE for offline first operation in areas with limited connectivity).
Geospatial data modelling for Bwindi’s terrain incorporated the framework of Obe & Hsu (2021), who demonstrate that “PostGIS extends PostgreSQL to support advanced geographic features, enabling efficient storage and querying of spatial data.” This is critical for the park’s geofencing requirement the system must verify that tourists are physically with Bwindi’s boundaries before granting access. The conceptual model represents this through geofence_boundary as a POLYGON in the PARK entity, enabling the ST_Contains() spatial query that checks user location against park boundaries. Similarly, location coordinates are stored as POINT geometry for distance calculations in proximity alerts, a feature that notifies tourists when they approach gorilla viewing areas within the park’s dense forest.
Multilingual content delivery, a requirement for Bwindi’s diverse visitor base of international tourists and local Uganda visitors, follows the guidance of Phrase (2024), which describes storing translations in “separate columns for each language rather than a single language-code column to avoid complex joins and maintain query performance.” In the conceptual model, entities such as CULTURAL NARRATIVE include title_en and title_local attributes, allowing Batwa elder’s stories to be preserved in Rukiga while remaining accessible to English-speaking tourists. This approach respects both cultural preservation goals and practical usability requirements.

4.6 Logical Database Design
Logical database design is the process of organizing system data into well-structured tables and defining relationships among entities to reduce redundancy and improve data integrity. For the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS), the logical database design was guided by normalization principles to ensure consistency, accuracy, and reliability of tourism information stored within the PostgreSQL database system.
The design process involved identifying key entities such as users, tourists, tour guides, wildlife sightings, cultural narratives, locations, tours, navigation paths, and park information, and organizing them into normalized relational tables. Relationships between entities were established using primary keys and foreign keys to support efficient data management, geospatial processing, offline synchronization, reporting, and analytics functionalities.
The normalization process ensured that the database achieved Third Normal Form (3NF) and Boyce-Codd Normal Form (BCNF), eliminating redundancy and update anomalies. All attributes were made atomic, partial dependencies were removed, and transitive dependencies were eliminated to maintain data integrity. For example, guide information is stored separately from tour session records, while junction tables such as animal_location and tour_participant were used to handle many-to-many relationships efficiently.
The logical database design for SIGTS consists of 35 normalized tables, including USER, tourist, tour_guide, it_manager, park, location, animal, sighting, cultural_narrative, tour_session, tour_participant, tour_route, navigation_path, feedback, notification, sync_queue, visitor_flow, and congestion_prediction. These tables support core system functionalities such as wildlife tracking, geofencing, interactive navigation, AI-powered recommendations, offline synchronization, tour management, analytics, and cultural storytelling.
4.7 Physical Database Design
Physical database design is the process of transforming the logical database structure into actual database tables, fields, data types, indexes, and storage structures within the database management system. The SIGTS physical design was implemented using PostgreSQL with PostGIS extensions to support spatial and geolocation data.

CHAPTER FIVE: SYSTEM IMPLEMENTATION, TESTING AND VALIDATION
5.0IntroductionThis chapter documents how the Smart Information Guide Tour System (SIGTS) was translated from design into a working application. It describes the technologies employed, the principal functions delivered to tourists, field guides, and park administrators, and the procedures used to test and validate data handled by the system.
5.1 System Implementation
Implementation followed a three-tier arrangement: a browser-based client served from the project frontend, a Node.js application programming interface backed by PostgreSQL with PostGIS extensions, and supporting services for email, optional large-language-model tour assistance, and real-time notifications. Development was carried out in modular stages so that authentication, catalogue content, mapping, sightings, guide scheduling, and administrative analytics could be integrated incrementally.
5.1.1 System technologies
The client layer comprises three applications in a pnpm monorepo: a Next.js 14 tourist PWA (Leaflet maps, next-pwa, IndexedDB offline queue), a Next.js guide app, and a React Admin IT portal. Shared utilities in @sigts/shared handle API calls and sync. Leaflet renders gates, trails, and sightings within the park boundary. A service worker caches selected assets so that tourists retain access to catalogue material when connectivity is weak inside the forest.
The server layer is built on Express.js running on Node.js. Representational State Transfer (REST) endpoints expose park content, user accounts, sightings, tour sessions, cultural narratives, geofence checks, feedback, and analytics. Socket.io broadcasts tour-session location updates; JSON Web Tokens secure authenticated sessions, with refresh-token rotation and configurable idle timeout. PostgreSQL stores relational data; PostGIS supports spatial queries for boundaries, visitor location context, and proximity to named places.
Technologies were selected for availability, maintainability within the student project timeline, and alignment with park operations. Open-source components reduced licensing cost while allowing the team to deploy the stack on modest hardware suitable for demonstration and pilot use at Bwindi Impenetrable National Park.
5.2 System Functionality
5.2.1 Authentication and role-based access
The system distinguishes tourists, registered guides, and information-technology managers. Visitors authenticate before accessing personalised dashboards, saved bookmarks, and sighting submission. Guides receive schedules, shift controls, and guest lists tied to assigned treks. IT managers access predictive analytics, backup controls, cultural narrative publishing, and operational summaries. Registration, password recovery, and email verification flows were implemented so that accounts could be provisioned without manual database edits.
5.2.2 Map guidance and location services
The map module combines park boundary geometry, location records, trek routes, and recent wildlife sightings. Users may search for gates and sectors, measure approximate distances, and receive contextual notes when geolocation is available. Geofence logic supports demonstrations of on-site versus off-site access policies required for certain features during the pilot.
5.2.3 Visitor information, culture, and tour assistance
Tourists browse a species catalogue enriched with conservation status, habitat notes, and safety guidance drawn from park-approved content. Cultural narratives present community stories with publishing controls managed by authorised staff. The Ask the Park AI page in the tourist Next.js app demonstrates tour-help chat; it is wired to POST /api/tourist/ai-query (planned backend handler). Until that route ships, visitors rely on curated FAQs and catalogue endpoints described in Section 4.6.3.1.
5.2.4 Guide operations and administrative analytics
Guides record sightings, review preparation checklists, and monitor alerts for unusual observations. The IT dashboard aggregates visitor-flow indicators, satisfaction scores, sightings trends, congestion forecasts, and operational status for scheduled reports, scheduled SQL analytics jobs, and backup activity. These facilities support day-to-day park management decisions described in earlier chapters of this report.
5.3 System Testing
System testing confirmed that tourists, guides, and IT managers could complete essential tasks without critical defects. Manual inspection, structured walkthroughs, and automated checks were applied on the demonstration build. Table 5.2 summarises representative test cases and outcomes.
5.3.1 User testing
User acceptance testing involved representative users performing scripted tasks (login, browse wildlife facts, view map, submit feedback). Observers recorded pass/fail and task time. Participants rated statements on a five-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree). Mean usability score across core tasks was 4.2/5, indicating strong acceptance of verified content and navigation.

CHAPTER SIX: SUMMARY, LIMITATIONS, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
6.0 IntroductionThis chapter closes the technical account of the Smart Information Guide Tour System. It restates what the project achieved, notes operational challenges observed during development and testing, acknowledges limitations of the study, and offers recommendations for future deployment at Bwindi Impenetrable National Park.
6.1 Achievements
The project delivered a working prototype that supports tourist discovery of biodiversity and cultural content, map-based orientation within the park context, guide-facing workflow tools, and administrative analytics grounded in live database records. Offline caching and queued synchronization were implemented so that brief loss of connectivity in the field does not prevent users from reading cached material or deferring sightings until the device reconnects. Security controls—including role separation, token-based sessions, and production configuration checks—were incorporated to align the build with responsible pilot deployment.
6.2 Challenges
Several challenges emerged during implementation. Reliable connectivity inside and around the park remains uneven; features that depend on continuous access to the application programming interface degrade gracefully but cannot replace on-site ranger briefings. Maintaining accurate species and narrative content requires ongoing collaboration with Uganda Wildlife Authority and community partners. Optional tour-help capabilities depend on external language-model services when enabled; without them, the system relies on rule-based responses that are dependable yet less conversational. Server availability remains essential for authentication, synchronisation, and analytics used by IT staff.
6.3 Limitations of the study
The study was constrained by time, equipment, and access to field respondents. User testing drew on a modest sample drawn largely from academic and demonstration settings rather than a full season of tourist traffic across all four sectors of the park. Hardware for load testing at scale was limited, so concurrent-usage behaviour under peak trekking periods was inferred rather than measured exhaustively. Some specification items—such as native mobile packaging, SMS-based multi-factor authentication, and full ranger-dispatch integration—were documented as future work rather than completed within the project window.
6.4 Recommendations
Park management and the development team are advised to pilot SIGTS with trained guides and a controlled group of visitors before wider release. Short training sessions should cover map use, sighting etiquette, cultural content sensitivity, and realistic expectations for tour-help responses. Content stewards should establish a schedule for reviewing species records, FAQs, and safety tips. Production hosting should use strong secrets management, routine database backups, and monitoring of server health. Continued investment in connectivity at sector offices would increase the value of real-time analytics for operations staff.
6.5 Conclusion
The Smart Information Guide Tour System was undertaken to improve how visitors prepare for and experience Bwindi Impenetrable National Park while giving guides and administrators shared tools for information delivery and monitoring. The implemented prototype demonstrates that a web-centred architecture with spatial data, offline tolerance, and role-aware interfaces can support this objective in a demonstrable form. The technologies chosen proved adequate for academic delivery and pilot demonstration, provided that content governance and infrastructure maintenance remain active responsibilities of the park. Further refinement—particularly in mobile distribution, extended field evaluation, and integration with official permit and ranger systems—was designed to determine whether SIGTS transitions from a project outcome to a sustained operational service.

Interview Guide: Smart Information Guide Tour System Study
We are researchers conducting a study aimed at developing a Smart Information Guide Tour System to enhance the educational experience and information accuracy for visitors within Bwind Impenetrable national park. The purpose of this professional consultation is to gather insights on current information delivery practices, challenges, and the functional requirements for a technology web-based guide tour system. The information collected was used strictly for academic research and system development to support conservation, education and the tourism sector.
Your participation in this interview helped inform the design of a system that seeks to empower guides, ensure visitor satisfaction, and promote accurate cultural and ecological storytelling.
